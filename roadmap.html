Recovery to Purpose
Roadmap

A steady, realistic path in recovery, outdoor leadership, and service

Written for Johnny

The Vision

To build a life rooted in recovery, nature, brotherhood, and service. A life where the outdoors becomes a place for men to regain strength, clarity, trust, and direction.

Adventure becomes a place of healing. Nature becomes a classroom. Your story becomes a guide.

Recovery becomes something worth protecting — not just maintaining.

The Most Important Thing

Your recovery always comes first.

If any part of this path threatens your stability, the path pauses.

Nothing meaningful is lost by protecting sobriety.

The mission can wait. Your foundation cannot.

"Build a life so stable and meaningful that helping others becomes natural."

Stage 0

Foundation: Recovery & Strength

Now through Year 1

Prioritize meetings, support, honesty, sleep, routine, and physical health.

Use hiking, walking, fishing, paddling, skiing, snowshoeing, camping, or strength training as stabilizing practices. Let nature be part of recovery before it becomes part of any mission.

The Phoenix or similar sober active communities can be especially valuable here. North Cottage — the sober dry houses on campus — offer the next step in structure and responsibility.

This stage is not preparation for the path. This stage IS the path.

Stage 1

Identity: Become the Outdoor Leader

Year 1 through Year 2

You are no longer only recovering. You begin to become the person people trust to get them outside.

  • 1

    Invite one or two sober friends on easy, familiar outings.

  • 2

    Keep groups small, simple, safe, and consistent.

  • 3

    Take photos, jot down what worked, and notice what helps people open up.

  • 4

    Build trust first. Marketing can come later.

You don't need a business yet. You need repetition, relationship, and proof — to yourself — that you love doing this.

Stages 2 & 3

Apprenticeship & Professional Entry

Year 2 – Year 4

Stage 2 — Apprenticeship

  • Look for volunteer or entry roles in outdoor, experiential, or recovery-oriented programs
  • Safety training: Wilderness First Aid, CPR/AED, Wilderness First Responder, Leave No Trace
  • Learn risk management, group facilitation, crisis response, and professional boundaries
  • Watch experienced people closely — their judgment matters as much as their credentials

Stage 3 — Professional Entry

  • Entry points: peer support, recovery support, behavioral health or residential support, outdoor-program staff
  • Some begin in community-based recovery support; others start inside treatment programs or sober living
  • Massachusetts recovery-coach standards are evolving — check the current state pathway
  • Choose work that protects recovery rather than work that drains it
Stages 4 & 5

Credentials & Integration

Year 3 and Beyond

Stage 4 — Credentials

  • If you want the clinical route, verify current LADC requirements with Massachusetts BSAS
  • If you want the community route, strong safety practices and steady leadership may be enough
  • Credentials should support the mission — not replace it
  • Do not chase titles at the expense of the life you are trying to preserve

Stage 5 — Integration

  • This could look like a community outdoor group, a peer-led recovery initiative, or a role inside an established program
  • You may stay local and small. You may grow something larger. Either is valid.
  • The real achievement is not scale — it is building something honest, stable, and useful
  • Your story doesn't exist in many places. You could write the playbook others follow.

Two Roads — Both Are Real

Path A — Clinical / Credentialed Path B — Community / Informal
Credentials Yes, if you want to provide licensed counseling or bill clinically. No, though basic safety training is strongly recommended.
Admin load Moderate to high: documentation, ethics, compliance, supervision. Low: coordination, communication, safety planning, consistency.
Income Can become a stable profession over time, depending on role and licensure. May stay volunteer-based or only cover expenses — and can still matter deeply.
Time to start Longer. Usually unfolds in stages over several years. Now. You can begin by leading one safe walk.
What it gives Clinical impact, career stability, and a formal lane to serve. Community, purpose, belonging, and lives changed through steady presence.

A Practical Next 90 Days

If the goal is simply to begin, this is enough.

1

Stay close to the recovery practices that are already working.

2

Plan one small outdoor outing and keep it easy.

3

Invite only a few people you trust.

4

Write down what happened and how it felt.

5

Repeat it.

→

Only after consistency appears should you think about websites, structure, training, or formal pathways.

None of this requires you to have everything figured out. It requires you to take the next honest step.
