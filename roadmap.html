Stage 1

The Foundation — Just Show Up

Now through Year 1

What This Stage Is About

You don't need a business plan right now. You need repetition, relationship, and proof — to yourself — that you love doing this. Stage 1 is about showing up consistently as the person who brings others outside. Nothing more.

What You're Actually Building

  • Your sobriety and personal stability — this is your true compass
  • Trust with other men in recovery by being reliable and present
  • A personal identity as the guy who takes people outdoors
  • Stories, photos, and early testimonials for when you're ready
  • Physical conditioning and confidence in your own body

Practical Actions

  • Invite 2–3 sober friends on a short walk — no agenda, just move
  • Pick places you already know and love. Keep it simple.
  • Start taking photos. These become your website content.
  • Buy the domain jvoadventures.com (done!) — let it sit until ready
  • If it feels right, attend a local hiking or outdoor club just to observe

How You'll Know You're Ready for Stage 2

When you've done 10–15 outings and you're still excited. When people keep asking "when's the next one?" When it doesn't feel like work — it feels like your life.

Stage 2

Making It Real — Community and Early Structure

Year 1 — Year 2

What This Stage Is About

You now have a small track record. It's time to make JVO Adventures a real thing — not a business yet, but a recognizable community. You build a web presence, start planning trips with intention, and let people know you exist.

Website and Online Presence

  • Launch jvoadventures.com — keep it simple. A photo, your story, an upcoming trip.
  • Write a short, honest "About" page. Your recovery story IS your mission statement.
  • Start an Instagram or Facebook showing your outings. Authenticity over polish.
  • Create a simple mailing list so people can hear about upcoming trips

Trip Planning

  • Plan one small group outing per month — 4–8 people maximum
  • Mix abilities. Not everyone is in great shape, and that's part of the therapy.
  • Beginner-friendly: short hikes, local fishing spots, snowshoe walks
  • Build in rest stops and conversation. That's where the real work happens.
  • Consider a seasonal approach: spring hikes, summer kayaking, fall foliage, winter snowshoeing

Connecting to the Recovery Community

  • Introduce yourself to North Cottage staff — tell them what you're doing informally
  • Attend open AA/NA meetings in your area. Let your identity as an outdoorsman be known.
  • Reach out to halfway houses and sober living homes as a peer volunteer
  • Don't pitch a business. Just offer a hike. Let the word spread naturally.

Important Considerations

  • At this stage, keep it informal and free or low-cost — no liability concerns yet
  • Begin researching liability insurance for outdoor guides — know what's ahead
  • Look into basic Wilderness First Aid (WFA) certification — a weekend course, very accessible
Stage 3

Getting Credentialed — The Academic Path Begins

Year 2 — Year 3

What This Stage Is About

This is where the long-term vision becomes a formal commitment. You begin the educational pathway toward becoming a licensed counselor. This doesn't mean stopping what you're doing — it runs alongside it. But it gives your future work clinical weight, ethical grounding, and professional opportunity.

Addiction Counseling Licensure in Massachusetts

Massachusetts offers several pathways. The most accessible entry point is the Licensed Alcohol and Drug Counselor (LADC) credential. Requirements include a bachelor's degree or equivalent, 270 hours of supervised practicum, and a written exam. Research the Massachusetts Board of Substance Use Disorder Professionals for current specifics.

Degree Options Worth Exploring

  • Associate or Bachelor's in Human Services — many online, flexible programs
  • Bachelor's in Psychology or Counseling with an addiction focus
  • Master's in Mental Health Counseling (for full LMHC licensure — the gold standard)
  • Certificate programs in Substance Use Disorder Counseling — faster, targeted

Adventure Therapy Specific Training

  • Association for Experiential Education (AEE) — look into their certification programs
  • Outdoor Behavioral Healthcare (OBH) Council — the leading professional association in this field
  • Wilderness First Responder (WFR) — the professional-grade outdoor emergency credential
  • Leave No Trace (LNT) Instructor certification — respected, accessible, inexpensive

What You're Building Professionally

The combination of a counseling license plus adventure therapy training is rare and powerful. Most programs either have clinical staff who don't go outside, or outdoor guides who aren't clinicians. The person who bridges that gap is exceptionally valuable and difficult to replace.

Revenue Begins

  • Start charging modest fees for trips once you have basic credentials and insurance
  • Apply to be a paid outdoor recreation facilitator at treatment centers
  • Look into peer recovery coach certification — Massachusetts has a formal pathway
  • Consider offering one-on-one outdoor coaching sessions at an hourly rate
Stage 4

The Clinician-Guide — Running Real Programs

Year 3 — Year 5

What This Stage Is About

You now have credentials, experience, and a reputation. JVO Adventures becomes a legitimate therapeutic service. You design and deliver structured outdoor recovery programs, contract with treatment facilities, and build sustainable income doing work you love.

Program Design

  • Create a signature multi-week outdoor recovery program — 6 or 8 sessions, progressive difficulty
  • Develop themed experiences: men's resilience retreats, transition-to-civilian programs, early sobriety adventure series
  • Design curriculum that intentionally mirrors recovery concepts: trust, risk, challenge, community, achievement
  • Build in debrief and reflection time after each outing — this is where the clinical value lives

Partnership Opportunities

  • Contract with residential treatment centers to provide outdoor programming for clients
  • Partner with halfway houses to offer weekly group outings as part of structured sober living
  • Explore Veterans services — outdoor therapy for veteran recovery is significantly underfunded
  • Approach Employee Assistance Programs (EAPs) for corporate wellness retreats
  • Collaborate with schools and courts for at-risk youth programming

Business Structure

  • Form an LLC — protects you personally, looks professional to institutional clients
  • Obtain proper liability insurance for adventure therapy programs
  • Create intake forms, participant agreements, and basic clinical documentation
  • Build a fee schedule with sliding scale options for those in early recovery
  • Consider applying for nonprofit status if community service is the primary mission

What Your Week Might Look Like

Two or three individual counseling sessions. One group outdoor program for a local treatment center. Weekend day trip for the JVO Adventures community. Time for your own recovery, health, and rest. A sustainable rhythm that you built yourself.

Stage 5

The Legacy — Building Something Larger Than Yourself

Year 5 and Beyond

What This Stage Is About

JVO Adventures is no longer just you. It's a model, a brand, and potentially an organization that can help many more people than you could reach alone. This stage is about multiplication — training others, expanding reach, and cementing a legacy in men's recovery.

Possible Directions

  • Train and certify peer outdoor guides in recovery — create the model you wish had existed for you
  • Develop an apprenticeship program where men in recovery learn to lead others outdoors
  • Build a residential outdoor recovery retreat — multi-day immersive wilderness experiences
  • Write a book or create training materials on peer-led adventure therapy
  • Build a nonprofit arm of JVO Adventures to provide subsidized programming for those who can't afford it
  • Speak at recovery conferences — your story and model are genuinely unusual and compelling

The Unique Contribution You Could Make

There are very few peer-counselors-turned-adventure-guides in this field. Most programs are institutional. What you would represent — a man in long-term recovery who built a business around bringing other men outside and healing — that story doesn't exist in many places. You could write the playbook that others follow.

None of this requires you to have it all figured out.
It requires you to take the next walk.
